# gendered-timeuse-slr

**PRISMA-Based Systematic Literature Reviews on Gendered Time Use and Labour Inequality**

This repository documents the methodology and outputs of two systematic literature reviews co-authored with Dr Motilal Mahamallik and Dr Harchand Ram at the Institute of Development Studies (IDS), Jaipur, conducted June–August 2025.

## Overview

Two parallel PRISMA-compliant systematic reviews examining:

1. **Review 1: Distribution of Paid Work, Unpaid Labour, and Leisure by Gender**
Synthesising the global literature on how time is allocated across market work, household production, care work, and leisure, with a focus on gender differentials across countries and life stages.

2. **Review 2: Gender Gaps in Sleep Time and Leisure Inequality**
Sleep time is underused as a welfare measure in economics. This review examines the literature on sleep and leisure inequality and how care burdens shape recovery time differently for women and men across countries.

Both reviews are in preparation for journal submission (2026).

## Methods

### Search Strategy
- Databases searched: Web of Science, Scopus, EconLit, PsycINFO, Google Scholar
- Search terms: time use, time allocation, unpaid work, care work, gender, labour inequality, sleep inequality, leisure
- Date range: 2000–2025
- Languages: English

### Screening and Deduplication
- **Total records identified: 4,328**
- Two-stage deduplication pipeline using Zotero and Rayyan QCRI
- Title and abstract screening followed by full-text review
- PRISMA flow diagram documenting inclusion and exclusion at each stage

### Synthesis and Mapping
- Thematic synthesis of included studies
- Bibliometric network visualisations produced using **VOSviewer** to map research clusters
- Clusters identified: gendered care burden literature, time poverty literature, sleep inequality literature, labour supply and time use modelling

## Key Findings (Preliminary)

- Women's time poverty persists across countries and life stages, particularly around caregiving responsibilities
- The gap between measured leisure time and genuine leisure is significant — fragmented leisure associated with on-call care is systematically undercounted
- Sleep inequality has received limited attention in the economics literature relative to its welfare implications
- Household type (joint vs nuclear) and employment sector are significant predictors of caregiving load distribution
